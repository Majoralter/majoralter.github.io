In Interstellar, Cooper defines Murphy’s Law as:

>Whatever can happen will happen.

Traditionally, Murphy’s Law states that “Anything that can go wrong, will go wrong.” It carries a pessimistic tone, suggesting an inevitability of failure. However, I see Cooper’s version as a broader and more neutral interpretation. What can happen will happen—not because of good or bad forces, but simply because it is within the realm of possibility.

Given infinite time, all conceivable events will occur. A set of twins can be born, a person could die, a cat might suffer a stroke, a ball could smash through a window, and a man may propose to his partner—all of these things can, and at some point, will happen. Whether we perceive them as fortunate or unfortunate is irrelevant to their inevitability.

It’s natural to assign meaning to events, especially when misfortune strikes. Komarov didn’t want his parachute to fail, but failure was a possibility—so, unfortunately, it happened. Not because of an external force or "bad luck," but because the probability existed.

People often believe that outcomes are personal, that good things happen because they are earned and bad things because they are cursed. But the reality is more indifferent. Every event, no matter how significant or trivial, is just the manifestation of possibilities playing out. No experience is unique forever—history repeats, and what happens to one person today has likely happened to someone else before.

This isn’t to say that life is meaningless or that effort doesn’t matter. Our responses to events shape our futures, influencing what will happen next. But trying to rationalize why bad things happen is ultimately fruitless—they simply do.

And what of good things? It’s comforting to believe that hard work ensures success, but effort doesn’t always guarantee reward. Some people work just as hard and never reach their goals. This isn’t unfair; it’s just probability at work.

Existence is neither predetermined nor entirely random—it operates on a spectrum of cause and effect, probability and inevitability. What can happen, will happen.
